1. **Ellis et al. (2025)** - "The Halo Effect: Perceptions of Information Privacy Among Healthcare Chatbot Users" - J Am Geriatr Soc - **Unaccepted**
   - **Exclusion Reason**: While privacy perception research is important, if the literature does not link to **how interface design affects privacy risk perception** (transparency dimension), it cannot directly support the research argument. Ellis et al. (2025) provides empirical evidence that older adults underestimate privacy risks due to trust in healthcare institutions (halo effect), but does not analyze how specific interface design elements (such as transparency mechanisms, persuasive design patterns) shape or exploit this perception bias.
   - **Research Question Impact**: This exclusion has important implications for the research question's argumentative logic. In the original plan, Ellis et al. (2025) was considered "core literature directly supporting the research question" because it provides empirical evidence of older adults' privacy perception bias. However, Round 2 evaluation emphasizes that the core of the research question is "**gaps in interface design practices**," not merely the existence of perception bias. Therefore, we need to find literature that **simultaneously covers privacy perception bias and interface design impact**, or adjust the research question formulation to clearly distinguish "perception problems" from "design problems."
   - **Alternative Approach**: Need to find literature focusing on "how interface design affects older adults' privacy risk perception" or research on "how design interventions correct perception biases."
